Rooted and Rising

Matcha lattes, matcha cheesecakes, matcha donuts, matcha milk teas, matcha facemasks, and even matcha perfumes: the uses of matcha seem endless. This powdered green tea has exploded in popularity worldwide, loved for both its vibrant flavor and supposed health benefits. But matcha is far more than just a wellness trend.

Long before it became a global phenomenon, matcha was at the heart of Japanese tea ceremonies—a ritualized practice known as chanoyu that emphasizes presence, preparation, and respect. The powdered tea was introduced to Japan from China during the Tang dynasty, but it was the Zen Buddhist monks who embraced matcha for its meditative properties. Preparing and drinking matcha became a deeply spiritual act, a way to center oneself and find calm in simplicity.

Traditional matcha preparation is precise and deliberate. The vibrant green powder is sifted, scooped with a bamboo ladle (chashaku), and whisked in hot water using a chasen until a frothy, velvety consistency is achieved. This method isn’t just about taste—it’s about intention. Each step honors centuries of practice and invites mindfulness, turning an everyday beverage into a moment of reflection.

In recent years, matcha has surged into mainstream cafes and skincare aisles alike. Its distinct flavor and bold color have made it a favorite for creative culinary twists, from matcha soft serve to savory sauces. Simultaneously, health-conscious consumers have latched onto its antioxidant-rich profile, associating it with energy boosts, glowing skin, and improved focus. But in the rush to capitalize on the matcha craze, its origins are often diluted.

As matcha continues to grow in popularity, there's a growing opportunity to connect more deeply with its heritage. Learning about its origins, supporting ethical sourcing, and practicing traditional methods can help preserve its rich history. Matcha isn’t just a trend—it’s a cultural practice that continues to evolve while honoring where it came from.
